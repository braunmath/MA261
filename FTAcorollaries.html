    Let \(p\) be a prime. If \(a\) and \(b\) are natural numbers such that \(p\mid ab\text{,}\) then \(p\mid a\) or \(p\mid b\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Write the prime factorizations \(a=p_1^{\alpha_1}\cdots p_k^{\alpha_k}\) and \(b=q_1^{\beta_1}\cdots q_t^{\beta_t}\text{.}\) Then we have
    \begin{equation*} ab = p_1^{\alpha_1}\cdots p_k^{\alpha_k}q_1^{\beta_1}\cdots q_t^{\beta_t} \, . \end{equation*}
    If \(p\) divides the product, then \(p\) must appear in the list of primes in the factorization of \(ab\text{.}\) Therefore, \(p\) must be a divisor of one or both of \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\)

    Corollary 3.4.3.

    Suppose that \(b=p_1^{\beta_1}\cdots p_k^{\beta_k}\text{.}\) If \(a\) is any divisor of \(b\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} a = p_1^{\alpha_1}\cdots p_k^{\alpha_k} \end{equation*}
    Where each \(\alpha_i\leq \beta_i\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    If \(p^\alpha\) divides \(a\) and \(a\mid b\text{,}\) then \(p^{\alpha} \mid b\text{.}\) Thus, any power of a prime dividing \(a\) must also divide \(b\text{.}\)

    We next define the greatest common divisor and least common multiple of two numbers, and express these quantities in terms of prime factorizations.
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 3.4.4.

    Let \(a\) and \(b\) be natural numbers. The greatest common divisor of \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\) denoted \(\gcd(a,b)\text{,}\) is given by the largest natural number dividing both \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\) If \(\gcd(a,b)=1\text{,}\) we say that \(a\) and \(b\) are coprime. The least common multiple of \(a\) and \(b\) , denoted \(\lcm(a,b)\text{,}\) is given by the smallest natural number divisible by both \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\)

    The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic is also the key ingredient in many classical proofs of irrationality. Here are three examples.
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 3.4.8.

    The value \(\sqrt{2}\) is irrational.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    Suppose for a contradiction that \(\sqrt{2}=a/b\) for natural numbers \(a\) and \(b\) where the fraction is fully reduced; in other words, \(a\) and \(b\) are coprime. Then squaring both sides, we have that
    \begin{equation*} 2=a^2/b^2 \end{equation*}
    and therefore \(2b^2=a^2\text{.}\) However, this implies that there are an odd number of \(2\)s in the prime factorization of the left side, while there are an even number in the prime factorization on the right. This is a contradiction, and therefore \(\sqrt{2}\) is not rational.

    Theorem 3.4.10.

    The value \(\log_2(3/2)\) is irrational.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    Suppose for contradiction that \(\log_2(3/2)=a/b\) for coprime numbers \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\) Then we have that \(\log_2((3/2)^b)=a\text{,}\) which implies that
    \begin{equation*} (3/2)^b=2^a \, . \end{equation*}
    This implies that
    \begin{equation*} 3^b=2^{a-b} \, , \end{equation*}
    which is impossible by the uniqueness of prime factorization, yielding the desired contradiction.
