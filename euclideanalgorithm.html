    Section 4.2 The Euclidean Algorithm

    We started the previous section by looking at linear Diophantine equations of the form
    \begin{equation*} ax + by = c\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(a\text{,}\) \(b\text{,}\) and \(c\) are given integers and we want to find integer solutions \(x,y\text{.}\) What we observed is that there seems to be a connection between the solvability of this equation and the greatest common divisor of \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\) which we will establish in detail later in this chapter. In this section, we will develop the theory of greatest common divisors using the division algorithm, which we will need for our study of linear Diophantine equations.
    ðŸ”—
    Recall that in PropositionÂ 3.4.6 we showed how to compute the greatest common divisor of two numbers if you know the prime factorizations of those numbers. Unfortunately, it is famously difficult to find the prime factorization of large numbers. In fact, the security of many modern cryptographic systems relies on this difficulty, see https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer_factorization for more information. So, we need to find a better way to compute greatest common divisors without having to factor the numbers first.
    ðŸ”—
    Fortunately, this was first accomplished by the ancient Greek mathematician Euclid around 300 BC. This is the same Euclid who wrote The Elements, one of the most influential works in the history of mathematics, and whose proof of the infinitude of primes we saw previously in the course.
    ðŸ”—
    The idea of the Euclidean algorithm is to apply the division algorithm repeatedly. Why does this help us? It is because the division algorithm is closely related to the greatest common divisor, as the following lemma shows.
    ðŸ”—

    Lemma 4.2.1.

    Let \(a\) and \(b\) be positive integers and use the division algorithm to write
    \begin{equation*} a = bq + r \end{equation*}
    for some integers \(q\) and \(r\) with \(0 \leq r\lt b\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} \gcd(a,b) = \gcd(b,r)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Letâ€™s look at an example. Suppose \(a=15\) and \(b=6\text{.}\) Using the division algorithm, we can write
    \begin{equation*} 15 = 6 \cdot 2 + 3\text{.} \end{equation*}
    According to the lemma, we have
    \begin{equation*} \gcd(15,6) = 3 = \gcd(6,3)\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    We first show that any common divisor of \(a\) and \(b\) is also a common divisor of \(b\) and \(r\text{.}\) Let \(d\) be a common divisor of \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} a = dm_1 \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} b = dm_2 \end{equation*}
    for some integers \(m_1\) and \(m_2\text{.}\) Substituting into the equation
    \begin{equation*} a = bq + r \end{equation*}
    gives
    \begin{equation*} dm_1 = dm_2 q + r\text{,} \end{equation*}
    which implies
    \begin{equation*} r = d(m_1 - m_2 q)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Thus, \(d\) divides \(r\text{,}\) and so \(d\) is a common divisor of \(b\) and \(r\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    We next show that any common divisor of \(b\) and \(r\) is also a common divisor of \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\) Let \(d\) be a common divisor of \(b\) and \(r\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} b = dm_2 \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} r = dm_3 \end{equation*}
    for some integers \(m_2\) and \(m_3\text{.}\) Substituting into the equation
    \begin{equation*} a = bq + r \end{equation*}
    gives
    \begin{equation*} a = dm_2 q + dm_3\text{,} \end{equation*}
    which implies
    \begin{equation*} a = d(m_2 q + m_3)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Thus, \(d\) divides \(a\text{,}\) and so \(d\) is a common divisor of \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Since the sets of common divisors of \(a\) and \(b\) and of \(b\) and \(r\) are the same, their greatest common divisors must also be the same.

    So, how does the Euclidean algorithm work? It works by allowing us to replace the pair \((a,b)\) by the smaller pair \((b,r)\) without changing the greatest common divisor. We can repeat this process until the remainder is zero. At that point, the other number is the greatest common divisor. Letâ€™s do an example before we write down the algorithm formally.
    ðŸ”—
    Suppose \(a=2542\) and \(b=1056\text{.}\)
    • Using the division algorithm, we have
      \begin{equation*} 2542 = 1056 \cdot 2 + 430\text{.} \end{equation*}
      Thus,
      \begin{equation*} \gcd(2542,1056) = \gcd(1056,430). \end{equation*}
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—

      \begin{equation*} 1056 = 430 \cdot 2 + 196\text{.} \end{equation*}
      So, \(\gcd(1056,430) = \gcd(430,196)\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—

      \begin{equation*} 430 = 196 \cdot 2 + 38\text{,} \end{equation*}
      so \(\gcd(430,196) = \gcd(196,38)\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—

      \begin{equation*} 196 = 38 \cdot 5 + 6\text{,} \end{equation*}
      so \(\gcd(196,38) = \gcd(38,6)\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • Next, we have
      \begin{equation*} 38 = 6 \cdot 6 + 2\text{,} \end{equation*}

    Letâ€™s now give a formal description of the Euclidean algorithm.
    ðŸ”—

    Algorithm 4.2.6. The Euclidean Algorithm.

    Suppose that \(a\) and \(b\) are positive integers with \(a \geq b\text{.}\) The Euclidean algorithm computes \(\gcd(a,b)\) as follows:
    1. Use the division algorithm to write
      \begin{equation*} a = bq_1 + r_1 \end{equation*}
      for some integers \(q_1\) and \(r_1\) with \(0 \leq r_1 \lt b\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    2. If \(r_1 = 0\text{,}\) then stop and output \(b\) as the greatest common divisor. Otherwise, continue to the next step.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    3. Use the division algorithm to write
      \begin{equation*} b = r_1 q_2 + r_2 \end{equation*}
      for some integers \(q_2\) and \(r_2\) with \(0 \leq r_2 \lt r_1\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    4. If \(r_2 = 0\text{,}\) then stop and output \(r_1\) as the greatest common divisor. Otherwise, continue to the next step.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    5. Continue this process, at each step using the division algorithm to divide the previous divisor by the previous remainder, until a remainder of zero is obtained. The last nonzero remainder is the greatest common divisor \(\gcd(a,b)\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    The correctness of the algorithm follows by repeated application of LemmaÂ 4.2.1.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Letâ€™s think a little bit about the practical efficiency of the Euclidean algorithm. Intuitively, if we have a step where the quotient \(q\) is large, then we quickly reduce the size of the numbers we are working with. On the other hand, if the quotients are all small, then we reduce the size of the numbers more slowly. So, what happens in the case where every quotient is as small as possible, that is, every quotient is equal to 1?
    ðŸ”—
    Suppose that \(\gcd(a,b)=1\) and that every quotient in the Euclidean algorithm is equal to 1. Suppose that with our first step we have
    \begin{equation*} a = b \cdot 1 + r_1\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and with our second step we have
    \begin{equation*} b = r_1 \cdot 1 + r_2\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and with our third step we have
    \begin{equation*} r_1 = r_2 \cdot 1 + r_3\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and so on. Eventually, we will reach a final step where
    \begin{equation*} r_{n-1} = r_{n} \cdot 1 + 0\text{,} \end{equation*}
    so that \(r_{n} = \gcd(a,b) = 1\text{.}\) If we rewrite all of these equations, we get that
    \begin{equation*} r_{n} = 1\text{,} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} r_{n-1} = 1 + 0 = 1\text{,} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} r_{n-2} = 1 + 1 = 2\text{,} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} r_{n-3} = 2 + 1 = 3\text{,} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} r_{n-4} = 3 + 2 = 5\text{,} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} r_{n-5} = 5 + 3 = 8\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and so on. In other words, we start with the numbers \(1\) and \(1\text{,}\) and each new number is the sum of the previous two numbers.

    The numbers that arise from this process are the following.
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 4.2.8.

    Let \(F_0 = 1\) and \(F_1 = 1\text{,}\) and for each integer \(n \geq 2\text{,}\) let
    \begin{equation*} F_n = F_{n-1} + F_{n-2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The resulting sequence of numbers \(F_0, F_1, F_2, \ldots\) are called the Fibonacci numbers.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Our calculation above proves the following theorem.
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 4.2.9.

    If the Euclidean algorithm is applied to two relatively prime positive integers \(a\) and \(b\) with \(a \geq b\) and every quotient in the algorithm is equal to \(1\text{,}\) then \(a\) and \(b\) are consecutive Fibonacci numbers.
