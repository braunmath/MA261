    Theorem 4.3.1. BÃ©zout Identity.

    Let \(a,b\in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}\) with not both \(a = 0\) and \(b = 0\text{.}\) There exist \(x,y\in \mathbb{Z}\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \gcd(a,b) = ax + by \, . \end{equation*}

    Letâ€™s think about this equation for a moment.
    1. We know that the greatest common divisor of \(a\) and \(b\) divides both \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\) thus it divides \(ax+by\text{.}\) So, any number of the form \(ax+by\) must be a multiple of \(\gcd(a,b)\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
    2. The BÃ©zout identity says that there is some choice of \(x\) and \(y\) such that \(ax+by\) is exactly equal to \(\gcd(a,b)\text{.}\) In other words, the smallest possible integer that one could get from the expression \(ax+by\text{,}\) which is \(\gcd(a,b)\text{,}\) is always obtainable using some integers \(x\) and \(y\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
    This is a remarkable fact, and it has many applications in number theory and beyond. In this section, we will give three different proofs of the BÃ©zout identity, each highlighting a different proof technique.
    ðŸ”—
    For our first proof, we will use the Euclidean algorithm to construct the integers \(x\) and \(y\) needed for the identity.
    ðŸ”—

    Proof of BÃ©zout Using the Euclidean Algorithm.

    Suppose that the Euclidean algorithm applied to \(a\gt b\) produces \(n\) nonzero remainders, i.e., that we have the following sequence of equations:
    \begin{align*} a \amp =q_1b+r_1\\ b \amp =q_2r_1+r_2\\ r_1 \amp =q_3r_2+r_3\\ r_2 \amp = q_4r_3 + r_4 \\ \amp \ldots\\ r_{n-2} \amp =q_nr_{n-1}+r_n\\ r_{n-1} \amp =q_{n+1}r_n+0 \end{align*}
    Thus, we have that \(\gcd(a,b) = r_n\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    We can write \(r_2\) in terms of \(a\) and \(b\) by rearranging the first two equations:
    \begin{align*} r_1 \amp = a - q_1b\\ r_2 \amp = b - q_2r_1 = b - q_2(a - q_1b) = -q_2a + (1 + q_1q_2)b \end{align*}
    Similarly, we can write \(r_3\) in terms of \(a\) and \(b\) by substituting our expressions for \(r_1\) and \(r_2\) into the third equation:
    \begin{align*} r_3 \amp = r_1 - q_3r_2 = (a - q_1b) - q_3(-q_2a + (1 + q_1q_2)b)\\ \amp = (1 + q_2q_3)a + (-q_1 - q_3 - q_1q_2q_3)b \end{align*}
    For simplicity, letâ€™s denote the coefficients of \(a\) and \(b\) in these expressions as follows:
    \begin{align*} r_2 \amp = X_2a + Y_2b \amp \text{where } \amp X_2 = -q_2, \ Y_2 = 1 + q_1q_2\\ r_3 \amp = X_3a + Y_3b \amp \text{where } \amp X_3 = 1 + q_2q_3, \ Y_3 = -q_1 - q_3 - q_1q_2q_3 \end{align*}
    Similarly, we can write \(r_4\) in terms of \(a\) and \(b\) by substituting our expressions for \(r_2\) and \(r_3\) into the fourth equation:
    \begin{align*} r_4 \amp = r_2 - q_4r_3 = (X_2a + Y_2b) - q_4(X_3a + Y_3b)\\ \amp = (X_2 - q_4X_3)a + (Y_2 - q_4Y_3)b\\ \amp = X_4a + Y_4b \end{align*}
    where in the last equation we have defined \(X_4 = X_2 - q_4X_3\) and \(Y_4 = Y_2 - q_4Y_3\text{.}\) Continuing in this manner, we can express each remainder \(r_k\) as \(r_k = X_ka + Y_kb\) for some integers \(X_k, Y_k \in \mathbb{Z}\text{.}\) In particular, we have that \(r_n = X_na + Y_nb\) for some integers \(X_n, Y_n \in \mathbb{Z}\text{.}\) Since \(r_n = \gcd(a,b)\text{,}\) we have found integers \(X_n\) and \(Y_n\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \gcd(a,b) = aX_n + bY_n \, . \end{equation*}
    This completes the proof of the BÃ©zout identity using the Euclidean algorithm.

    We will next look at a proof of the BÃ©zout identity using the well-ordering principle, which is completely different from the previous proof. The difference is that the proof using the Euclidean algorithm is constructive: it shows us how to find the integers \(x\) and \(y\text{.}\) In contrast, the proof using the well-ordering principle is non-constructive: it shows us that such integers must exist, but does not provide a method for finding them.
    ðŸ”—

    Proof of BÃ©zout Identity using the well-ordering principle.

    Consider the set of all positive integers that can be expressed in the form \(ax + by\) for some integers \(x\) and \(y\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} S = \{ax + by : x,y \in \mathbb{Z} \text{ and } ax + by \gt 0 \} \, . \end{equation*}
    Observe that \(S\) is non-empty since, for example, we can take \(x = 1\) and \(y = 1\) to get \(a+b \gt 0\text{.}\) Thus, by the well-ordering principle, \(S\) has a smallest element. Our goal is to show that \(\gcd(a,b)\) is the smallest element of \(S\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Call the smallest element of \(S\) by the name \(d\text{.}\) If we divide \(a\) by \(d\text{,}\) we get a quotient \(q\) and a remainder \(r\) such that
    \begin{equation*} a = qd + r \end{equation*}
    with \(0 \leq r \lt d\text{.}\) We know that there exist integers \(x_0\) and \(y_0\) such that
    \begin{equation*} d = ax_0 + by_0 \, . \end{equation*}
    Substituting this into the equation for \(a\text{,}\) we have:
    \begin{align*} a \amp = q(ax_0 + by_0) + r\\ \amp = a(qx_0) + b(qy_0) + r \end{align*}
    Rearranging, we find that:
    \begin{equation*} r = a(1 - qx_0) + b(-qy_0) \, . \end{equation*}
    Since \(r\) is expressed in the form \(ax + by\) for some integers \(x\) and \(y\text{,}\) it follows that if \(r \gt 0\text{,}\) then \(r \in S\text{.}\) But this contradicts the minimality of \(d\) since \(r \lt d\text{.}\) Therefore, we must have \(r = 0\text{,}\) which means that \(d\) divides \(a\text{.}\) By a similar argument, we can show that \(d\) divides \(b\text{.}\) Thus, \(d\) is a common divisor of \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    It follows that
    \begin{equation*} d \leq \gcd(a,b)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    However, we also know that \(\gcd(a,b)\) divides both \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\) and thus divides \(ax_0 + by_0=d\text{.}\) Therefore, \(\gcd(a,b)\) divides \(d\text{,}\) which implies that
    \begin{equation*} \gcd(a,b) \leq d\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Combining these inequalities, we conclude that \(d = \gcd(a,b)\text{.}\) This completes the proof.

    The third proof we will look at is similar in spirit to the proof using the Euclidean algorithm, but it is written as an inductive proof using only the division algorithm.
    ðŸ”—

    Proof of BÃ©zout Identity using Induction.

    We will prove the BÃ©zout identity by strong induction on \(\min(a,b)\text{.}\) For the base cases, suppose first that \(\min(a,b) = 0\text{.}\) Without loss of generality, assume that \(b = 0\text{.}\) Then \(\gcd(a,b) = \gcd(a,0) = a\text{.}\) We can write \(a = a \cdot 1 + 0 \cdot 0\text{,}\) so the BÃ©zout identity holds. Second, suppose that \(\min(a,b) = 1\text{.}\) Without loss of generality, assume that \(b = 1\text{.}\) Then \(\gcd(a,b) = \gcd(a,1) = 1\text{.}\) We can write \(1 = a \cdot 0 + 1 \cdot 1\text{,}\) so the BÃ©zout identity holds.
    ðŸ”—
    For the inductive step, assume that the BÃ©zout identity holds for all pairs of non-negative integers \((a',b')\) with \(\min(a',b') \lt n\) for some \(n \geq 2\text{.}\) Now consider a pair of non-negative integers \((a,b)\) with \(\min(a,b) = n\text{.}\) Without loss of generality, assume that \(a \geq b\text{.}\) By the division algorithm, we can write \(a = qb + r\) for some integers \(q \geq 1\) and \(0 \leq r \lt b\text{.}\) Note that \(\min(b,r) \lt n\) since \(r \lt b \leq a\text{.}\) By the inductive hypothesis, there exist integers \(x_1\) and \(y_1\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \gcd(b,r) = bx_1 + ry_1 \, . \end{equation*}
    Since \(\gcd(a,b) = \gcd(b,r)\) and \(r=a-qb\text{,}\) we have:
    \begin{align*} \gcd(a,b) \amp = bx_1 + (a - qb)y_1\\ \amp = ay_1 + b(x_1 - qy_1) \end{align*}
    Thus, setting \(x = y_1\) and \(y = x_1 - qy_1\text{,}\) we have found integers \(x\) and \(y\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \gcd(a,b) = ax + by \, . \end{equation*}
    This completes the inductive step and thus the proof of the BÃ©zout identity.

    We will end with a corollary that strengthens our understanding of the greatest common divisor.
    ðŸ”—

    Corollary 4.3.7.

    If \(d\) divides both \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\) then \(d\) divides \(\gcd(a,b)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    Use the BÃ©zout identity to write \(\gcd(a,b) = ax + by\) for some integers \(x\) and \(y\text{.}\) Since \(d\) divides both \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\) it divides \(ax\) and \(by\text{.}\) Therefore, \(d\) divides the sum \(ax + by\text{,}\) which is equal to \(\gcd(a,b)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    This corollary tells us that not only is the greatest common divisor the largest divisor of \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\) but every divisor of \(a\) and \(b\) must also be a divisor of the greatest common divisor.
