    Section 5.6 Fermat’s Little Theorem

    The next topic we will consider is a result known as "Fermat’s Little Theorem". Pierre de Fermat was a mathematician in the 17th century who extensively studied number theory, and is perhaps best know for his "Last Theorem". Fermat’s little theorem is a key result in number theory that is a major ingredient in the study of primality testing and encryption algorithms, which you can learn more about in the course MA 340.
    🔗
    We will begin with the following experiments using either a calculator on your phone or using SageMath. Suppose that \(p\) is a prime number, and suppose \(a\) is a positive integer that is relatively prime to \(p\text{.}\) In symbols, \(p\nmid a\text{.}\) Is there a value \(x\) that consistently gives
    \begin{equation*} [a]^x=[1] \, ? \end{equation*}
    In other words, is there a value of \(x\) that will always guarantee
    \begin{equation*} a^x\equiv 1 \bmod p \, ? \end{equation*}

      🔗

    • Second, we see that it is hard to check this for all integers. In particular, if \(p\) and \(a\) are both extremely large, then they cannot be stored in the memory of a computer. Thus, if we think that \(x=p-1\) always produces \([1]\text{,}\) we need to prove it.
      🔗
      🔗
    This leads us to our main result.
    🔗

    Theorem 5.6.2. Fermat’s Little Theorem.

    If \(p\) is prime and \(a\) is a positive integer such that \(p\) does not divide \(a\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} [a]^{p-1} = [1] \, . \end{equation*}
    Equivalently,
    \begin{equation*} a^{p-1}\equiv 1 \bmod p \, . \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    We will give two proofs of Fermat’s little theorem. Our first proof relies on properties of units in \(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}\text{.}\) We will use the notation \(A\setminus B\text{,}\) read as "A setminus B", to denote the elements of \(A\) that are not in \(B\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Proof of Fermat’s Little Theorem using units.

    We begin our proof with the claim that every non-zero element \([a]\in \mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}\) is a unit, since every such \(a\) has \(\gcd(a,p)=1\text{.}\) Thus, for any such \([a]\text{,}\) the function \(M_a\) that sends \([b]\in \mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}\) to \([a][b]\) is an invertible function from \(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}\setminus \{[0]\}\) to itself. In other words,
    \begin{equation*} M_a:\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}\setminus \{[0]\} \rightarrow \mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}\setminus \{[0]\} \end{equation*}
    is a function that is both one-to-one and onto. Therefore, \(M_a\) rearranges the elements of \(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We prove this claim as follows. First, suppose that for \([b]\) and \([b']\) we have \([a][b]=[a][b']\text{.}\) Then multiplying both sides by \([a]^{-1}\) we obtain \([b]=[b']\text{,}\) and thus the function is one-to-one. Second, we want to show that \(M_a\) is onto, i.e., that every element of \(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}\) is mapped onto by \(M_a\text{.}\) Suppose that \([b]\in\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}\text{.}\) Then we know that \([a]^{-1}[b]\in\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}\text{.}\) So,
    \begin{equation*} M_a([a]^{-1}[b])=[a][a]^{-1}[b]=[b] \, , \end{equation*}
    showing that \([b]\) is mapped onto by \(M_a\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Thus, we see that
    \begin{equation*} \{[1],[2],[3],\ldots,[p-1]\}=\{[a][1],[a][2],[a][3],\ldots,[a][p-1]\} \, . \end{equation*}
    Hence, we have that the product of all the elements in the first set is equal to the product of all the elements in the second set, i.e.,
    \begin{equation*} [1][2][3]\cdots [p-1] = [a][1][a][2][a][3]\cdots [a][p-1] \, . \end{equation*}
    Multiplying both sides by
    \begin{equation*} [1]^{-1}[2]^{-1}[3]^{-1}\cdots [p-1]^{-1} \end{equation*}
    we obtain
    \begin{equation*} [1]=[a]^{p-1} \end{equation*}
    and this completes the proof.
    🔗
    🔗

    Our second proof uses a combinatorial argument, i.e., a proof based on counting.
    🔗

    Combinatorial proof of Fermat’s Little Theorem.

    We will prove that for any positive integer \(a\) we have
    \begin{equation*} a^p\equiv a \bmod p \, , \end{equation*}
    and thus in the special case where \(\gcd(a,p)=1\text{,}\) we can cancel \(a\) from both sides to obtain our desired formula
    \begin{equation*} a^{p-1}\equiv 1 \bmod p \, . \end{equation*}
    Observe that \(a^p\) counts the number of length \(p\) "words" that we can write using \(a\) letters. For example, if \(p=3\) and \(a=3\text{,}\) then there are \(3^3=27\) words of length three using the three letters \(\{a,b,c\}\text{:}\)
    \begin{gather*} aaa,aab,aac\\ aba,abb,abc\\ aca,acb,acc\\ baa,bab,bac\\ bba,bbb,bbc\\ bca,bcb,bcc\\ caa,cba,cca\\ cba,cbb,cbc\\ cca,ccb,ccc \end{gather*}
    We need to show that
    \begin{equation*} p\mid a^p-a\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Observe that \(a^p-a\) is the number of words remaining after we delete all the single-letter words. In our above example, if we remove \(aaa\text{,}\) \(bbb\text{,}\) and \(ccc\) from the list then there are \(3^3-3\) words remaining. To show that \(p\) divides \(a^p-a\text{,}\) we need to gather the remaining words into groups of size \(p\text{.}\) The way we will do this is to gather the words when they agree by a cyclic shift, i.e., starting from a word create a list of \(p\) words by repeatedly taking the last letter and moving it to the first position. For example, when \(p=7\) we will group the words in the following list, where the next word is obtained by moving the last letter to the front of the next word:
    \begin{gather*} abcabac\\ cabcaba\\ acabcab\\ bacabca\\ abacabc\\ cabacab\\ bcabaca \end{gather*}
    The length of our words is prime, and therefore we never see a repeated word in this list (since that would create a divisor of \(p\)). Thus, this method gathers all of the \(a^p-a\) words into groups of size \(p\text{,}\) and hence \(p\) divides \(a^p-a\text{,}\) as desired.
    🔗
    🔗
