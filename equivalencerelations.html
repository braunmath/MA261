    Section 5.1 Equivalence Relations

    Throughout mathematics, we rely on the remainder of a division operation. For example, even and odd numbers are numbers that have remainder \(0\) and \(1\text{,}\) respectively, when divided by \(2\text{.}\) We proved that there are infinitely many primes that have remainder \(3\) when divided by \(4\text{.}\)
    🔗
    More practically, we rely on remainders all the time in our daily life. When we measure time, we have \(60\) seconds in a minute, \(60\) minutes in an hour, \(24\) hours in a day, and \(7\) days in a week. So, for example, if it is \(10:35\)am, then in \(75\) minutes it will be \(11:50\)am. We know this because \(75\) minutes is \(1\) hour and \(15\) minutes, and adding that to \(10:35\)am gives us \(15\) minutes after \(11:35\)am. If it is a Tuesday, then \(9\) days later it will be a Thursday, since \(9\) has remainder \(2\) when divided by \(7\) and two days after Tuesday is Thursday. When we do these types of calculations with time, we are taking remainders using the numbers \(60\text{,}\) \(24\text{,}\) and \(7\text{.}\)
    🔗
    When we measure angles, we have \(360\) degrees in a circle. So, when a snowboarder does a trick that involves spinning around by \(720\) degrees, we know that they have spun around twice and are facing the same direction as when they started, since \(720\) has remainder \(0\) when divided by \(360\text{.}\) On the other hand, if they do a \(540\) degree spin, then they have spun around once and a half times, and are facing the opposite direction as when they started, since \(540\) has remainder \(180\) when divided by \(360\text{.}\)
    🔗
    In this way, we see that there are certain days that are equivalent; every Tuesday differs from every other Tuesday by a multiple of \(7\) days. Every May differs from every other May by a multiple of \(12\) months. A snowboarder is facing the same direction as every other snowboarder who has gone through a spin that differs from theirs by a multiple of \(360\) degrees.
    🔗
    These are all examples of equivalence relations, which are a fundamental concept in formal mathematics. We will develop the theory of equivalence relations in this section, and then we will use it to define a new type of arithmetic on a finite set of numbers \(\{0,1,2,\ldots,n-1\}\) called modular arithmetic.
    🔗
    Motivated by the examples above, we will begin with the formal definition of an equivalence relation.
    🔗

    Definition 5.1.1.

    A binary relation on a set \(S\) is a subset of the Cartesian product \(S \times S:=\{(a,b) : a,b \in S\}\text{.}\) We write \(a \cong b\) to indicate that the ordered pair \((a,b)\) is in the binary relation.
    🔗
    🔗
    In other words, a binary relation on \(S\) is a collection of ordered pairs of elements of \(S\text{.}\) We say that two elements \(a,b \in S\) are related if \(a \cong b\text{.}\) An equivalence relation is a special type of binary relation.
    🔗

    Checkpoint 5.1.2.

    What binary relation on \(\mathbb{R}\) is represented by the graph of the function \(f(x)=x^2+1\text{?}\) (Recall that the graph of a function represents a specific collection of ordered pairs in \(\mathbb{R}\times \mathbb{R}\text{.}\))
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 5.1.3.

    Let \(S\) be a set. An equivalence relation on \(S\) is a binary relation \(\cong\) on \(S\) such that for all \(a,b,c \in S\text{,}\) we have:
    • \(a \cong a\) (reflexivity)
      🔗
    • if \(a \cong b\text{,}\) then \(b \cong a\) (symmetry)
      🔗
    • if \(a \cong b\) and \(b \cong c\text{,}\) then \(a \cong c\) (transitivity).
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 5.1.4.

    Show that the binary relation on \(\mathbb{R}\) defined by the ordered pairs \((x,x^2+1)\) is not an equivalence relation.
    🔗
    🔗
    Here is a key observation: Many of the things students have been trained to think of as "equalities" are actually "equivalences". Let’s look at an example.
    🔗

    Example 5.1.5.

    Consider the set of all lines in the plane. We can define a relation on this set by saying that two lines are related if they are parallel. This relation is an equivalence relation, which we can check as follows.
    • First, since every line is parallel to itself, reflexivity is satisfied.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Second, note that if a line is parallel to another line, then the second line is parallel to the first line. Thus, symmetry is satisfied.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Third, if a line is parallel to a second line, and the second line is parallel to a third line, then the first line is parallel to the third line. Thus, transitivity is satisfied.
      🔗
      🔗
    So, parallelism is an equivalence relation on the set of lines in the plane.
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 5.1.6.

    Discuss the example above. Does it make sense? Why or why not?
    🔗
    🔗
    A very important example of an equivalence relation is the definition of when two fractions are equivalent. We will focus on this case next. Students are taught that \(\frac{1}{2} = \frac{2}{4}\text{.}\) However, this is not actually an equality! For example, if I have two apples and I take one of them, this is not the same as if I have four apples and I take two of them. So, one out of two is not the same as two out of four. However, we can define that the fractions \(\frac{a}{b}\) and \(\frac{c}{d}\) are equivalent if \(ad = bc\text{,}\) and we write
    \begin{equation*} \frac{a}{b} \cong \frac{c}{d}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    With this definition, we see that \(\frac{1}{2} \cong \frac{2}{4}\text{,}\) and by tradition we write this as an equality.
    🔗

    Checkpoint 5.1.7.

    Prove that the binary relation defined above on fractions satisfies reflexivity, symmetry, and transitivity.
    🔗
    🔗
    What the previous exercise shows is that the set of all fractions
    \begin{equation*} \{\frac{a}{b}:a,b\in \mathbb{Z},b\neq 0\} \end{equation*}
    breaks up into classes of fractions that are equivalent to each other. What we "know" from elementary school is that each fraction is in a unique class. In formal mathematics, this requires a proof! It turns out that this represents a general property of equivalence relations.
    🔗

    Definition 5.1.8.

    For each element \(a \in S\text{,}\) we define the equivalence class of \(a\) to be the set
    \begin{equation*} [a] = \{x \in S : x \cong a\} \, . \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    For example, the equivalence class of the fraction \(\frac{3}{5}\) is the set
    \begin{equation*} \{\frac{a}{b}:3b=5a \text{ with } a,b\in \mathbb{Z}, b\neq 0\} \, . \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Checkpoint 5.1.9.

    What is the equivalence class of the line \(y=2x+3\) under the parallelism equivalence relation?
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 5.1.10.

    An equivalence relation \(\cong\) on a set \(S\) partitions \(S\) as a disjoint union of the equivalence classes for \(\cong\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    First, note that every element \(a \in S\) is in its own equivalence class, since \(a \cong a\text{.}\) Thus, the union of all the equivalence classes is equal to \(S\text{.}\) Second, we will show that if two equivalence classes have a common element, then they are equal. So, let \([a]\) and \([b]\) be two equivalence classes, and suppose that they have a common element \(x\text{.}\) Then, by definition of equivalence class, we have \(a \cong x\) and \(x \cong b\text{.}\) By transitivity of the equivalence relation, we have \(a\cong b\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 5.1.11.

    Discuss the proof above. Does it make sense? Why or why not?
    🔗
    🔗
    Let’s return to our example of equivalent fractions. Here is an interesting question: Suppose we pick two different representatives from each of two different equivalence classes of fractions and then we add them together. How do we know that these two addition processes result in fractions representing the same equivalence class?
    🔗
    For example, if we take a representative of the equivalence class of \([\frac{1}{2}]\text{,}\) such as \(\frac{2}{4}\text{,}\) and a representative of the equivalence class of \([\frac{2}{3}]\text{,}\) such as \(\frac{6}{9}\text{,}\) and we add them together, we get \(\frac{2}{4}+\frac{6}{9}=\frac{42}{36}\text{.}\) So, we have that
    \begin{equation*} [\frac{1}{2}]+[\frac{2}{3}] = [\frac{42}{36}]\text{.} \end{equation*}
    But, if we take different representatives of the same equivalence classes, such as \(\frac{3}{6}\) and \(\frac{4}{6}\text{,}\) and we add them together, we get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{3}{6}+\frac{4}{6}=\frac{7}{6}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    So, we have that
    \begin{equation*} [\frac{1}{2}]+[\frac{2}{3}] = [\frac{7}{6}]\text{.} \end{equation*}
    How do we know that \([\frac{42}{36}]\) and \([\frac{7}{6}]\) are always the same equivalence class? How do we know that we always get the same equivalence class regardless of which of the infinitely many choices of representatives we pick from the equivalence classes we are adding?
    🔗

    Theorem 5.1.12.

    The addition operation is well-defined on equivalence classes of fractions. In other words, if \(\frac{a}{b} \cong \frac{a'}{b'}\) and \(\frac{c}{d} \cong \frac{c'}{d'}\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \frac{a}{b}+\frac{c}{d} \cong \frac{a'}{b'}+\frac{c'}{d'}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    We have that \(\frac{a}{b} \cong \frac{a'}{b'}\) means that \(ab' = a'b\text{,}\) and \(\frac{c}{d} \cong \frac{c'}{d'}\) means that \(cd' = c'd\text{.}\) We need to show that
    \begin{equation*} (ad+cb)(b'd') = (a'd'+c'b')(bd)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    which is the required condition to establish that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{a}{b}+\frac{c}{d} \cong \frac{a'}{b'}+\frac{c'}{d'}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Because \(ab' = a'b\) and \(cd' = c'd\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} (ad+cb)(b'd') = adb'd' + cbb'd' = a'dbd' + c'bb'd = (a'd'+c'b')(bd) \, . \end{equation*}
    Therefore, the result holds.
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 5.1.13.

    Discuss the proof above. Does it make sense? Why or why not?
    🔗
    🔗
    The previous two theorems show that we can treat the equivalence classes of fractions as if they were independent objects, and we can add them together by selecting representatives of each class and get the same result regardless of which representatives we choose to add together. Thus, addition of equivalence classes of fractions is "well-defined". We will do something similar when we construct the modular arithmetic system in the next section.
    🔗

    Checkpoint 5.1.14.

    Discuss the statement above. Does it make sense? Why or why not?
