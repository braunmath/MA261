    Section 3.2 Infinitude of Primes

    The most important question about primes that was asked in ancient times is the following.
    ðŸ”—

    Question 3.2.1.

    How many primes are there? Are there only finitely many? Or are there infinitely many?
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Letâ€™s look at some data. Define \(\pi(n)\) to be the number of primes less than or equal to \(n\text{.}\) (Here \(\pi\) is the Greek letter corresponding to "p", not the number \(3.14\ldots\text{.}\)) So, for example,
    \begin{equation*} \pi(10)=4 \end{equation*}
    since \(2,3,5,7\) are the four primes less than or equal to \(10\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    The following code will plot a point at \([p,\pi(p)]\) for every prime \(p\) up to \(100\text{.}\) What happens if we change the upper bound to \(1000\text{?}\)
    ðŸ”—

M = 100
for i in range(M):
    if i in Primes():
        L.append([i,len(L)+1])
points(L,xmax=M,ymax=M)
    As was discovered thousands of years ago, the answer to our question is the following theorem, recorded in Euclidâ€™s Elements.
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 3.2.2.

    There are infinitely many primes.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    We will give two versions of Euclidâ€™s proof. The first is a constructive proof.
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    We will apply the following operation: given a set of primes \(p_1,\ldots,p_k\text{,}\) produce the number \(N=p_1p_2\cdots p_k+1\text{.}\) We will call this a Euclid step.
    ðŸ”—
    Note that \(p_1\) does not divide \(N\text{,}\) since dividing \(N\) by \(p_1\) leaves a remainder of \(1\text{.}\) Similarly, dividing \(N\) by \(p_2\) leaves a remainder of \(1\text{.}\) The same holds for every \(p_i\text{.}\) Therefore, \(N\) is a number that is not divisible by any of the primes we have, and by TheoremÂ 3.1.12, there is a new prime \(p_{k+1}\) that divides \(N\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Thus, starting with \(p_1=2\text{,}\) we obtain the prime \(2+1=3\text{.}\) From there, we can produce the prime \(2\cdot3+1=7\text{.}\) From there, we can produce the prime \(2\cdot 3 \cdot 7 +1=43\text{.}\) From there, we can produce the number \(2\cdot 3 \cdot 7 \cdot 43 +1= 1807 =13\cdot 139\text{.}\) We can pick either of \(13\) or \(139\) to add to our list of primes.
    ðŸ”—
    With each application of a Euclid step, we produce a new prime. Thus, this yields an algorithm for constructing an infinite sequence of prime numbers.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Next we will consider Euclidâ€™s original version of this proof, which is a proof by contradiction. In a proof by contradiction, you suppose that the result is false, and show that this leads to a contradiction, hence the result must be true.
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    Suppose for contradiction that there are only finitely many primes, call them \(p_1,\ldots,p_k\text{.}\) If we apply a Euclid step (as defined in the previous proof) to these primes, then this produces a new prime that is not in our list. This contradicts our assumption that there are only finitely many primes, and thus there must be infinitely many primes.
