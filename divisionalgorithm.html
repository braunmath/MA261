    When \(a,b,c\) are integers, the equation
    \begin{equation*} ax+by=c \end{equation*}
    is called a linear Diophantine equation. In general, a Diophantine equation is a polynomial equation with integer coefficients for which we study solutions that are integer values. A linear Diophantine equation is a linear equation with integer coefficients, where we want to find integer solutions. Diophantine equations are named after Diophantus of Alexandria, a Greek mathematician who made significant contributions to number theory.
    ðŸ”—

    Remark 4.1.3.

    A former University of Kentucky undergraduate math and classics double-major, Cyrus Hettle, conducted undergraduate research on Diophantusâ€™s work and published a nice article about it in the Journal of Humanistic Mathematics: Cyrus Hettle, "The Symbolic and Mathematical Influence of Diophantusâ€™s Arithmetica," Journal of Humanistic Mathematics, Volume 5 Issue 1 (January 2015), pages 139-166. Available at: https://scholarship.claremont.edu/jhm/vol5/iss1/8
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    From your investigations with the previous checkpoint, you might have been led to conjecture that the equation
    \begin{equation*} ax+by=c \end{equation*}
    has integer solutions \(x,y\) if and only if \(c\) is a multiple of the greatest common divisor of \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\) We will prove that this is true later in this chapter, but first we will develop a broad theory regarding both greatest common divisors and the solution of linear Diophantine equations. We will begin with the division algorithm, which is a simple but powerful tool in number theory.
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 4.1.4. The Division Algorithm.

    Let \(a\) and \(b\) be positive integers, i.e., \(a,b\in \mathbb{N}\text{.}\) Then there exist unique integers \(q\) and \(r\) such that
    \begin{equation*} a = bq + r \end{equation*}
    and \(0 \leq r \lt b\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Before we prove TheoremÂ 4.1.4, letâ€™s give some names to the quantities involved. We call \(q\) the quotient and \(r\) the remainder when \(a\) is divided by \(b\text{.}\) Observe that \(r=0\) if and only if \(b\) divides \(a\text{,}\) by the definition of divisibility.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 4.1.5.

    Let \(a=17\) and \(b=5\text{.}\) Then by the division algorithm, there exist unique integers \(q\) and \(r\) such that
    \begin{equation*} 17 = 5q + r \end{equation*}
    and \(0 \leq r \lt 5\text{.}\) In this case, we have \(q=3\) and \(r=2\text{,}\) since
    \begin{equation*} 17 = 5\cdot 3 + 2\text{.} \end{equation*}

    While it might seem clear when doing examples that we can always find such integers \(q\) and \(r\) given \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\) neither this nor the uniqueness part of the theorem is immediately obvious. An embodied proof of the existence and uniqueness of the division algorithm might go as follows: "Move up the number line until you find the biggest multiple of \(b\) below \(a\text{.}\) This gives you your multiple \(qb\) and the difference between it and \(a\) is equal to \(r\text{,}\) and this is obviously unique." There are several assumptions that are being made in this argument, first the use of the number line to visualize "moving up" among the multiples of \(b\) and second the "obvious" remainder statement. Letâ€™s look at what a purely formal proof looks like, relying on the well-ordering principle and careful logical arguments involving inequalities rather than visual intuition.
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    Consider the set
    \begin{equation*} S=\{j\in \mathbb{N}:jb\gt a\} \, , \end{equation*}
    which is the set of all positive multiples of \(b\) that are greater than \(a\text{.}\) We first want to show that this set is not the empty set. Since \(b\gt 0\text{,}\) we know that \((a+1)b\geq a+1\gt a\text{,}\) so \((a+1)\in S\text{.}\) Thus,
    \begin{equation*} S\neq \emptyset \, . \end{equation*}
    By the well-ordering principle PrincipleÂ 3.3.7, since \(S\) is a non-empty set of positive integers, it must have a smallest element; call this element \(t\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Write \(t=q+1\) for some \(q\in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}\text{,}\) and note that \(q \notin S\) by the minimality of \(t\text{.}\) Then we have
    \begin{equation*} (q+1)b = tb \gt a \geq qb \, . \end{equation*}
    Subtracting \(qb\) from each part of this inequality yields
    \begin{equation*} b\gt a - qb \geq 0 \, . \end{equation*}
    So, define \(r = a - qb\text{.}\) Then we have \(0 \leq r \lt b\) and
    \begin{equation*} a = bq + r \, . \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    To see that \(q\) and \(r\) are unique, suppose that there exist integers \(q'\) and \(r'\) such that
    \begin{equation*} a = bq' + r' \, , \end{equation*}
    with \(0 \leq r' \lt b\text{.}\) Then we have
    \begin{equation*} bq + r = bq' + r' \, . \end{equation*}
    Rearranging this equation yields
    \begin{equation*} b(q - q') = r' - r \, . \end{equation*}
    If \(q \neq q'\text{,}\) then \(|q - q'| \geq 1\text{,}\) so \(|b(q - q')| \geq b\text{.}\) But since \(0 \leq r,r' \lt b\text{,}\) we have \(|r' - r| \lt b\text{,}\) which is a contradiction. Thus, we must have \(q = q'\text{,}\) which implies that \(r = r'\) as well. Therefore, the integers \(q\) and \(r\) are unique.

    Letâ€™s put the division algorithm to work with several applications. The first is a method to test whether or not two numbers have the same remainder when divided by a positive integer \(d\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 4.1.8.

    Let \(d,m,n\in \mathbb{N}\text{.}\) The numbers \(m\) and \(n\) have the same remainder when divided by \(d\) if and only if \(d \mid (m - n)\text{.}\)

    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    We first prove that if \(m\) and \(n\) have the same remainder when divided by \(d\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} d \mid (m - n) \, . \end{equation*}
    By the division algorithm, there exist unique integers \(q_1,r_1\) such that
    \begin{equation*} m = d q_1 + r_1 \, , \end{equation*}
    with \(0 \leq r_1 \lt d\text{.}\) Similarly, there exist unique integers \(q_2,r_2\) such that
    \begin{equation*} n = d q_2 + r_2 \, , \end{equation*}
    with \(0 \leq r_2 \lt d\text{.}\) Since \(m\) and \(n\) have the same remainder when divided by \(d\text{,}\) we have \(r_1 = r_2\text{.}\) Thus, we have
    \begin{equation*} m - n = (d q_1 + r_1) - (d q_2 + r_2) = d (q_1 - q_2) \, . \end{equation*}
    Therefore,
    \begin{equation*} d \mid (m - n) \, . \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    We next prove the converse, that is, we prove that if
    \begin{equation*} d \mid (m - n) \, , \end{equation*}
    then \(m\) and \(n\) have the same remainder when divided by \(d\text{.}\) Since
    \begin{equation*} d \mid (m - n) \, , \end{equation*}
    there exists an integer \(k\) such that
    \begin{equation*} m - n = d k \, . \end{equation*}
    By the division algorithm, there exist unique integers \(q_1,r_1\) such that
    \begin{equation*} m = d q_1 + r_1 \, , \end{equation*}
    with \(0 \leq r_1 \lt d\text{.}\) Similarly, there exist unique integers \(q_2,r_2\) such that
    \begin{equation*} n = d q_2 + r_2 \, , \end{equation*}
    with \(0 \leq r_2 \lt d\text{.}\) Substituting these expressions for \(m\) and \(n\) into the equation
    \begin{equation*} m - n = d k \, , \end{equation*}
    we have
    \begin{equation*} (d q_1 + r_1) - (d q_2 + r_2) = d k \, . \end{equation*}
    Rearranging this equation yields
    \begin{equation*} r_1 - r_2 = d (k - (q_1 - q_2)) \, . \end{equation*}
    Since \(0 \leq r_1,r_2 \lt d\text{,}\) the only way for \(r_1 - r_2\) to be a multiple of \(d\) is if \(r_1 = r_2\) so that \(r_1-r_2=0\text{.}\) Therefore, \(m\) and \(n\) have the same remainder when divided by \(d\text{.}\)

    Our second application is related to prime numbers. We know that \(2\) is the only even prime number, since every other even number is divisible by \(2\text{.}\) An equivalent way to say this is that there are only finitely many primes that have remainder \(0\) when divided by \(2\text{.}\) What can we say about remainders of primes when divide by other numbers? Here is a classic result.
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 4.1.11. Dirichletâ€™s Theorem on Arithmetic Progressions.

    Given any two positive integers \(a\) and \(d\) that are relatively prime, i.e.,
    \begin{equation*} \gcd(a,d)=1 \, , \end{equation*}
    there are infinitely many primes that have remainder \(a\) when divided by \(d\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    We should look at various examples of this theorem to get a feel for what it is saying, and examples can be found on the wikipedia page for Dirichletâ€™s theorem.
    ðŸ”—
    A proof of Dirichletâ€™s theorem is far beyond the scope of this course, but it shows that primes are widely distributed across all possible remainders when divided by a positive integer \(d\text{,}\) as long as the remainder and the divisor are relatively prime. However, we will give a proof of a special case of this theorem that illustrates the main idea of the result.
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 4.1.12. Special case of Dirichletâ€™s theorem.

    There are infinitely many primes that have remainder \(3\) when divided by \(4\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    We will provide a proof given by Silvermanâ€™s book A friendly introduction to number theory, which is also given on the wikipedia page for Dirichletâ€™s theorem.
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    We will prove this in a similar manner to Euclidâ€™s proof that there are infinitely many primes. Specifically, we will construct an infinite sequence of primes that are of the form \(4k+3\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    We will need a simple lemma first: suppose that \(a_1,a_2,\ldots,a_n\) are positive integers where each \(a_i=4k_i+1\) for some integer \(k_i\text{.}\) Then the product
    \begin{equation*} A = a_1 a_2 \cdots a_n \, \end{equation*}
    is also of the form \(4k+1\) for some integer \(k\text{.}\) We prove this by observing that the product of two numbers of the form \(4k+1\) is also of the form \(4k+1\text{.}\) Specifically, if \(a=4k_1+1\) and \(b=4k_2+1\text{,}\) then
    \begin{align*} ab \amp = (4k_1+1)(4k_2+1) = 16k_1k_2 + 4k_1 + 4k_2 + 1 \\ \amp = 4(4k_1k_2 + k_1 + k_2) + 1 \, . \end{align*}
    Thus, using an argument by induction, it can be shown that the product of any finite number of integers of the form \(4k+1\) is also of the form \(4k+1\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Now, suppose that we have a list of prime numbers of the form \(4k+3\text{,}\) say \(p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_n\text{,}\) where none of these primes are equal to \(3\text{.}\) So, we can start with the prime \(7\text{.}\) Define
    \begin{equation*} N=4 p_1 p_2 \cdots p_n +3\, , \end{equation*}
    and observe that none of the primes \(3,p_1,p_2,\ldots,p_n\) divides \(N\text{.}\) Note that \(3\mid N\) would imply that \(3\mid 4\text{,}\) which is false, and \(p_i \mid N\) would imply that \(p_i \mid 3\text{,}\) which is also false since none of the \(p_i\) are equal to \(3\text{.}\) Thus, any prime factor of \(N\) must be of the form \(4k+1\) or \(4k+3\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    If every prime factor of \(N\) were of the form \(4k+1\text{,}\) then by our lemma, their product (which is equal to \(N\)) would also be of the form \(4k+1\text{,}\) which is a contradiction since \(N\) is of the form \(4k+3\text{.}\) Therefore, \(N\) must have at least one prime factor of the form \(4k+3\) that is not in our original list of primes.
    ðŸ”—
    We can then iterate this process to generate an infinite list of primes of the form \(4k+3\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    What is interesting is that Dirichletâ€™s theorem on arithmetic progressions contrasts with what we know about the distribution of the primes throughout the integers, which is given in the following famous result.
    ðŸ”—
    In order to state this theorem, we need to introduce one piece of notation. Given two functions \(f\) and \(g\) defined on the positive integers, we write
    \begin{equation*} f(x) \sim g(x) \, \end{equation*}
    to mean that
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = 1 \, . \end{equation*}
    If \(f\sim g\text{,}\) we say that \(f\) and \(g\) are asymptotically equivalent. In other words, the functions \(f\) and \(g\) are asymptotically equivalent if their ratio approaches \(1\) as \(x\) approaches infinity.
    ðŸ”—
    You have actually seen many examples in calculus of functions that are asymptotically equivalent. For example, consider the function \(f(x)=x^2 + 3x + 2\) and the function \(g(x)=x^2\text{.}\) Then we have
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^2 + 3x + 2}{x^2} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \left(1 + \frac{3}{x} + \frac{2}{x^2}\right) = 1 \, , \end{equation*}
    so \(f \sim g\text{.}\) Thus, for large values of \(x\text{,}\) the function \(f(x)\) behaves like the function \(g(x)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—

    Checkpoint 4.1.15.

    ðŸ”—
    It is important to note that the difference \(f-g\) of two asymptotically equivalent functions does not necessarily approach \(0\) as \(x\) approaches infinity. For example, the difference between our functions \(f\) and \(g\) is
    \begin{equation*} f(x) - g(x) = (x^2 + 3x + 2) - x^2 = 3x + 2 \, , \end{equation*}
    which approaches infinity as \(x\) approaches infinity. However, the ratio of \(f(x)\) to \(g(x)\) does approach \(1\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    So, two functions being asymptotically equivalent have the same rate of growth, meaning that the difference between them becomes insignificant compared to their size as \(x\) becomes very large.

    Theorem 4.1.17. Prime Number Theorem.

    Recall that \(\pi(n)\) denotes the number of primes less than or equal to \(n\text{.}\) Then we have
    \begin{equation*} \pi(n) \sim \frac{n}{\log(n)} \, , \end{equation*}
    where \(\log(n)\) is the natural logarithm of \(n\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    What this says is that the fraction of the numbers between \(1\) and \(n\) that are prime is approximately \(\frac{1}{\log{n}}\text{.}\)
